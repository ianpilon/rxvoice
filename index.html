02Why it matters

One authoritative source, delivered on the patient's terms

The information a patient needs is fragmented across a clinician-facing monograph, a plain-language patient leaflet, and the bottle label — and at each handoff, legibility or understanding can break down. RxVoice is the delivery layer that closes that gap.

ALegible for everyone, by voice

Small fonts and all-capital labels defeat older adults and people with vision loss. A spoken answer needs no reading at all — and the screen text is adjustable, high-contrast, and large by default.

Answers Legibility

BSearchable answers to real questions

"Can I take this with food?" "What do I do if I miss a dose?" The leaflet has the answer, but it's hard to find. RxVoice goes straight to the passage that answers the actual question, instead of handing over the whole document.

Answers Comprehensibility

CRead from the approved source, never invented

Trust is the product. RxVoice reads back the approved patient information and never improvises a dose or a warning. For anything urgent, it points the patient to their pharmacist, doctor, or emergency help — a graceful "ask your pharmacist" beats a confident wrong answer.

Answers Actionability & safety

The opening: Health Canada already requires structured plain-language patient information for every drug and is moving toward a machine-readable monograph. The authoritative content is becoming something software can ingest — and serve to patients, caregivers, and pharmacists from one source, without new regulation or larger labels.
